HIIT vs Traditional
Cardio

Arjun Mehta Cardio & Athletic Performance Coach

What Is HIIT?

High-Intensity Interval Training (HIIT) alternates between short bursts of maximum-effort exercise and brief recovery periods. A typical session lasts 20–30 minutes but pushes your cardiovascular system to near-maximum capacity during work intervals.

Examples include sprint intervals, Tabata protocols, cycling sprints, and circuit-style training where rest is tightly controlled to keep heart rate elevated.

"It's not about how long you train. It's about how hard you push when it counts."

What Is Traditional (Steady-State) Cardio?

Steady-state cardio — also called LISS (Low-Intensity Steady State) — involves maintaining a consistent, moderate pace for an extended duration. Think 45-minute jogs, cycling at a comfortable speed, or brisk walking. Your heart rate stays in the aerobic zone (60–70% of max) throughout.

It's less demanding on the nervous system, easier to recover from, and highly accessible for all fitness levels.

Calorie Burn: The Real Comparison

HIIT burns more calories per minute than steady-state cardio, and creates an "afterburn" effect (EPOC — excess post-exercise oxygen consumption) that keeps metabolism elevated for hours after training. A 25-minute HIIT session can match or exceed the total calorie burn of a 45-minute steady jog.

However, LISS allows for higher total weekly volume because it doesn't tax recovery as severely. More total sessions often means more total calories burned over a week.

Which Burns More Fat Overall?

  • HIIT: Higher calorie burn per session, stronger EPOC effect
  • LISS: Lower per-session burn but easier to do daily
  • Combined approach: Best of both worlds for most people
  • Training status matters: beginners benefit more from LISS initially

Which Should You Choose?

The answer depends entirely on your goals, recovery capacity, and schedule. HIIT is superior for time efficiency, cardiovascular fitness improvement, and metabolic conditioning. Steady-state cardio is better for active recovery days, beginners, and those with heavy strength training schedules who need to protect recovery.

  1. Goal: Fat loss fast → HIIT 3x/week + LISS 2x/week
  2. Goal: Endurance/running → Prioritise LISS with 1–2 HIIT sessions
  3. Goal: Maintain fitness → 2–3 LISS sessions weekly is enough
  4. Heavy lifter → LISS to protect strength training recovery
  5. Beginner → Start with LISS, introduce HIIT after 4–6 weeks
